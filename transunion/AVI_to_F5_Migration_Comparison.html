Refined Approach

Based on the scale of the environment and timeline considerations, an additional approach is being evaluated: using Terraform to codify VIP configurations and integrate with existing Harness pipelines. This provides a faster path to execution while still introducing automation and repeatability. This document compares all three approaches to support an informed decision.

Each approach represents a different balance of speed, standardization, and long-term supportability.
Approach 1  |  Manual / CLI  (Lift & Shift)
Recreate existing AVI VIPs on F5 BIG-IP via the GUI or CLI (TMSH). Business units then cut over their own workloads by flipping DNS. No pipeline automation, no standards enforcement.
Day 2 Experience: Adding or modifying a VIP is done through the F5 GUI or CLI (TMSH). No Terraform knowledge required. With administrative partitions, business units may be able to manage their own VIPs directly within their assigned partition.

✔  Benefits

  • Fastest path to VIP recreation and cutover
  • Lowest technical complexity per individual migration task
  • No dependency on Harness/Terraform skill ramp-up
  • Business units have direct control over their workloads
  • Fewest moving parts — lower technical risk during cutover

✘  Risks & Tradeoffs

  • Requires significant hands-on support — limited ability to execute at scale without additional assistance
  • No standards enforcement — configurations will diverge
  • No audit trail or change management via pipeline
  • No uniform management standard — each team may configure F5 independently
  • Operational burden lands on IT operations long-term
  • Self-service is possible via administrative partitions, but without guardrails or standards enforcement across tenants
  • Does not address TransUnion’s IaC/OneDev 2.0 strategic goal
  • Transitioning to IaC later means rework — mixing GUI-managed and code-managed VIPs creates configuration drift and operational risk
⚡ Key Tradeoff: Fastest path to the October 31 finish line. Accepts technical debt in exchange for speed, but requires significant hands-on support and a follow-on engagement to standardize.
Approach 2  |  Terraform + Harness  (Middle Ground)
Extract AVI configurations into Terraform code and provide each business unit with the code and instructions to integrate into their own Harness pipelines. VIPs are recreated programmatically with version-controlled state. Teams own their repos and pipelines.
Day 2 Experience: Adding or modifying a VIP is done by updating Terraform code in the team’s repo and running it through a Harness pipeline. Requires Terraform and Git familiarity. App teams can self-service if they have the skills; operations provides the initial scaffolding but is not a bottleneck.

✔  Benefits

  • Faster than full IaC blueprints — less ramp-up required
  • All VIP configurations captured in version-controlled code with state
  • Provides repeatable, auditable process for recreating VIPs
  • Establishes a Terraform/Harness foundation for future work
  • Operations is already comfortable with Terraform
  • Teams own their own repos and pipelines — modular and self-service

✘  Risks & Tradeoffs

  • Still requires Terraform + Harness ramp-up time
  • Does not fully enforce corporate security policy / standards
  • Requires a follow-on effort to add the blueprint layer for net-new workloads
  • Business units must integrate Terraform code into their own pipelines
  • Scope / LOE not yet sized — requires a revised SOW
  • Best practice is to commit to one management approach — VIPs managed via Terraform should not be modified manually to avoid state drift
⚡ Key Tradeoff: Balances speed with a degree of standardization. Gets VIPs recreated with state and leaves a usable code foundation. Likely requires a new or amended SOW to size properly.
Approach 3  |  Full IaC / Blueprint SOW  (Standards-First)
The current SOW approach. Build up to 20 standardized Harness blueprints consumed via OneDev 2.0, with RBAC, self-service pipelines, operational guides, and a full cutover playbook. End users never see back-office operations — operations manages everything as a service.
Day 2 Experience: Adding or modifying a VIP is done through the OneDev 2.0 self-service portal. Select a blueprint, fill in parameters, submit. No Terraform or F5 knowledge required — RBAC and standards are enforced automatically. Operations manages blueprints, not individual VIPs.

✔  Benefits

  • Fully enforces corporate security policy and standards
  • Self-service model for business units via Harness pipelines
  • RBAC controls limit blast radius of individual team changes
  • Long-term operational playbook and Day-1 readiness included
  • Supports the strategic IaC / OneDev 2.0 vision
  • Version-controlled, auditable infrastructure for all F5 workloads

✘  Risks & Tradeoffs

  • Longest ramp-up time — puts October 31 deadline at risk
  • Requires teams to learn Harness / IaC before migrating workloads
  • Highest upfront cost and engagement complexity
  • Terraform familiarity varies across teams — knowledge gaps possible
  • Workloads may have undocumented legacy configs that resist templating
  • Some VIPs will be exceptions not covered by the 20 blueprints
  • Requires full organizational commitment — all VIP management must flow through the blueprint model to maintain consistency and avoid drift
⚡ Key Tradeoff: Prioritizes long-term standardization and operational health. Highest business value but requires timeline flexibility beyond October 31 or a phased plan.
